## Added Case: Algiers Plague And The American Sanitary Ban

The earlier "Barbary captivity and plague" idea was too loose. The defensible version is narrower: Algiers experienced plague in 1817-1818, U.S. consul William Shaler proposed sanitary exclusion measures, and the case shows how plague, corsair mobility, naval presence, quarantine, and diplomacy became entangled after the Barbary Wars.

Safe public claim:

> The 1817-1818 Algiers case is best taught as sanitary exclusion and maritime security, not as proof that plague caused the Barbary Wars.

Primary/stronger support:

- Erik de Lange, "The Plague on Board: Algiers and the American Sanitary Ban of 1818," ERC Project Blog, 2017. Useful for the specific Shaler/sanitary-ban case and for the archive-derived framing, but not peer-reviewed.
- Marina Ini, "Quarantine, Diseased Geographies, and Cross-Cultural Encounters in the Eighteenth-Century Mediterranean," *The Historical Journal*, 2024. Stronger for Mediterranean quarantine, bills of health, and Barbary Coast provenance.
- U.S. Department of State, Office of the Historian, "Barbary Wars, 1801-1805 and 1815-1816." Useful only for broad U.S.-Barbary context.

Public-display caution:

- Do not say that plague decided the Barbary Wars.
- Do not say most American captives died of plague.
- Do not imply that Shaler's sanitary ban was epidemiologically sufficient by modern plague ecology.
- Keep the mechanism at the infrastructure level: ship boarding, sanitary exclusion, bills of health, quarantine, and port-health surveillance.

## Deferred: Convict Ships And Prison Hulks

The topic is likely worth a future module, but not this closeout pass. The source base needs tighter separation among:

- British prison hulks anchored in rivers and naval yards.
- Convict transport voyages to penal colonies.
- Prisoner-of-war hulks.
- Disease labels such as gaol fever, typhus, dysentery, cholera, scurvy, tuberculosis, and nonspecific fever.
- Early high mortality claims versus later normalized mortality estimates.

Candidate source handles:

- Digital Panopticon, "Convict Hulks" - useful public-history overview, but not enough alone for public burden claims.
- The National Archives, "Prison hulks" - useful for archival orientation, especially POW/hospital ships and disease conditions.

Safe future module would need a named hulk, date range, setting, burden denominator, and source IDs. Until then, keep deferred.

## Excluded: Hantavirus Cruise-Ship Claims

No supportable maritime outbreak case was identified. Hantaviruses belong in the Pathogen Atlas as reservoir/ecology evidence, not in the maritime atlas as a cruise-ship or closed-vessel case unless an official outbreak report exists.

Public-display caution:

- Do not use hantavirus as a speculative "shipboard ecology" analogy.
- Do not add map routes or cruise settings without official source support.

## Excluded: Cause-Specific Middle Passage Disease Proportions

The existing Middle Passage module is defensible because it distinguishes forced confinement, dehydration, malnutrition, heat, crowding, violence, waste, infection, and total voyage conditions. The tempting but weaker move is assigning exact shares of mortality to dysentery, smallpox, measles, scurvy, or other causes across the trade.

Relevant support:

- SlaveVoyages methodology and Eltis/Richardson support voyage data and total mortality patterns.
- Curtin and later quantitative scholarship support broad epidemiologic interpretation.
- Steckel and Jensen support a cautious claim that gastrointestinal disease was important, but not a universal cause-specific fraction for every voyage.

Public-display caution:

- Do not reduce the Middle Passage to generic "disease spread."
- Do not assign exact cause-specific percentages unless a named dataset/source supports that exact measure.
- Keep violence, dehydration, nutrition, coercion, and disease together without collapsing them into one cause.

## Deferred: Single-Ship Yellow Fever Import Reconstructions

The existing yellow-fever module is intentionally ecological: ships, warm ports, stored water, Aedes mosquitoes, nonimmune travelers, labor, and local conditions. A single-ship import reconstruction would need named-voyage evidence and careful handling of the importation-versus-local-origin debate.

Relevant support:

- NLM's "Politics of Yellow Fever in Alexander Hamilton's America" shows that 1793 Philadelphia contemporaries debated origin, treatment, ports, immigration, and sanitation.
- Downs supports yellow fever epidemiologic history.
- CDC/WHO support modern vector mechanism.

Public-display caution:

- Do not say every maritime yellow-fever outbreak was imported by ship.
- Do not turn 1793 Philadelphia into a solved single-route chain.
- Do not equate refugee arrival, ship arrival, mosquito ecology, and outbreak causation without case-level support.

## Final Note

The closeout result is deliberately sparse: one narrowed addition, four documented non-additions. That is the correct outcome for a public-facing scholarly teaching tool. The atlas is stronger when it refuses attractive but unsupported claims.
